## EMAIL BODY

---

Here's something that surprised me when I looked at the data on AI infrastructure spending.

Companies will spend $600-690 billion on AI infrastructure in 2026. GPUs are sold out for months. Lead times stretch 36 to 52 weeks. Every earnings call mentions capacity constraints.

And only 7% of companies achieve greater than 85% GPU utilization during peak periods.

We're in the middle of a supposed GPU shortage, and 93% of organizations can't fully use the hardware they already have.

That contradiction kept nagging at me. So I started digging into whether we've seen this pattern before.

We have. Three times.

---

### The factory that wouldn't change

The electric motor was invented in 1881. Factories started buying them immediately. The technology was clearly superior to steam engines. Managers were excited.

For 40 years, manufacturing productivity didn't budge.

Stanford economist Paul David studied what went wrong. Managers had replaced the steam engine with an electric motor but kept everything else identical. Same factory layout. Same drive shafts. Same pulleys. Same workflow.

They'd installed new technology without changing how they worked.

It took a full generation of new managers to realize: if each machine has its own motor, you don't need the drive shaft. And if you don't need the drive shaft, you can arrange machines by workflow instead of in a line along the shaft.

Only after they redesigned the entire factory did productivity finally explode.

Technology installation did not equal transformation. Organizational redesign did.

---

### The paradox economists couldn't explain

In 1987, Nobel laureate Robert Solow captured a puzzling reality: "We see computers everywhere except in the productivity statistics."

Companies were investing billions in computer systems. The technology was obviously powerful. For 15 years, the productivity data showed essentially no impact.

The resolution? A MIT Sloan study found that IT spending increased productivity only at companies that had redesigned their business processes around IT capabilities.

Companies that automated existing manual processes saw minimal gains. Companies that fundamentally rethought how work should flow saw substantial improvements.

Same pattern. Same lesson.

---

### Dark fiber, dark GPUs

The telecom parallel is the most uncomfortable one.

In the late 1990s, telecom companies laid over 80 million miles of fiber optic cable based on WorldCom's claim that internet traffic was doubling every 90 days. It wasn't. Traffic was doubling roughly once a year.

When the bubble burst in 2001, 85-95% of that fiber sat unused. They called it "dark fiber."

The financial carnage was severe. But here's the twist: that same dark fiber eventually became the foundation for broadband, cloud computing, and video streaming a decade later.

Short-term waste. Long-term necessity.

Now look at today's AI numbers:

- Only 8.6% of companies have AI agents deployed in production
- 95% of enterprise AI pilots deliver zero measurable return (not low return, zero)
- 42% of companies scrapped most AI initiatives in 2025, up from 17% the year prior
- RAND Corporation finds over 80% of AI projects fail, twice the rate of non-AI tech projects

We have a $600 billion infrastructure buildout meeting a 95% pilot failure rate.

That's not a technology problem. That's an organizational readiness crisis.

---

### The part that made me rethink everything

Harvard Business Review found that 70% of the challenges companies face rolling out AI are related to people and processes, not technical glitches.

70%. People and processes.

Yet when you look at where the money goes, the ratio is inverted. Infrastructure spending dominates. Only 37% of companies report significant investment in change management, training, or helping people actually integrate AI into their work.

And here's the stat that stopped me cold: one study of experienced developers found they were 19% slower with AI tools but estimated they were 20% faster.

We're not just struggling with AI adoption. We're struggling to know we're struggling.

---

### What history suggests happens next

Every historical precedent points to the same uncomfortable timeline:

- Electrification: 40 years to productivity gains
- Computerization: 15+ years of paradox
- Fiber optics: a decade from "waste" to foundation

The market is pricing in AI productivity gains on a 2-3 year timeline. History suggests organizational transformation takes decades, not quarters.

This doesn't mean the infrastructure buildout is wrong. You can't transform without it. The dark fiber became essential. The electric motors were necessary.

But there's a step between "we've built the infrastructure" and "we're capturing productivity gains" that everyone wants to skip: redesigning how we work.

That step takes far longer than anyone wants to admit.

---

### The question I keep coming back to

Everyone's asking: "Are we building enough AI infrastructure?"

Almost nobody's asking: "Can organizations transform fast enough to justify the infrastructure we're building?"

History says no. At least not on the timelines the market expects.

The real insight isn't that we should stop building. It's that we should stop pretending infrastructure equals transformation.

We're not investing in AI infrastructure. We're investing in organizational learning at massive scale. And that learning process is expensive, slow, and wasteful by nature.

AI infrastructure will transform how we work.

The question is whether it takes 5 years or 25.

History suggests 25. The market is betting on 5.

Someone's going to be wrong.
