# Writing Examples

This file contains reference examples and style guides to maintain a professional, counterintuitive, and engaging voice across platforms.

## Core Voice Philosophy

### The Veritasium Approach (Derek Muller)
**"Clarity numbs the mind, but confusion can crack it open."**

- Start by addressing what people *think* they knowâ€”then reveal the surprising truth
- Don't dump facts; challenge misconceptions first
- Share your own confusion openly: "This surprised me too..."
- Let the reader become a partner in discovery, not a passive recipient
- The goal: promote rationality and critical thinking without being preachy

### The Diary of a CEO Approach (Steven Bartlett)
**Deep, direct, and vulnerableâ€”without being polarizing.**

- Go to the heart of the matter quickly
- Pair honesty with vulnerabilityâ€”share your own challenges when asking hard questions
- Thorough preparation leads to confident delivery
- Let the conversation breathe; don't be rigid
- Balance boldness with genuine curiosity

## Style Guidelines

### What This Voice IS:
- **Counterintuitive but evidence-based** â€” challenge assumptions, but back it up
- **Curious, not preachy** â€” "Here's what I discovered" not "Here's what you should do"
- **Professionally warm** â€” approachable expertise, not cold authority
- **Positively provocative** â€” make people think without making them defensive
- **Specific over generic** â€” real examples, real numbers, real stories

### What This Voice IS NOT:
- Polarizing or controversial for attention's sake
- Condescending or know-it-all
- Generic motivational fluff
- Clickbait without substance
- Negative or fear-based

## Newsletter Examples

### Example 1: The Myth-Busting Opening
```

Section: Opening hook
Content:

Wake up early. Time block. Batch similar tasks. Weekly reviews.

My output increased. My stress increased more.

It took an embarrassing realization to understand why: I was optimizing for the feeling of being productive, not actual results.

Notes: Admits failure first, creates curiosity gap, promises counterintuitive insight
```

### Example 2: The Evidence Pivot
```
Subject: Why your to-do list is lying to you
Section: Main argument
Content:
Here's something that surprised me when I looked at the research:

Checking items off a to-do list triggers the same dopamine response as actually accomplishing something meaningful.

Your brain can't tell the difference between "sent 47 emails" and "shipped the project."

This explains a lot.

It explains why I could end days feeling accomplished but having nothing to show for it. It explains why busywork is so seductive. It explains why the people who get the most done often have the shortest to-do lists.

The solution isn't abandoning listsâ€”it's being honest about what belongs on them.

Before adding something, I now ask: "If this was the only thing I did today, would I be satisfied?"

Most things fail that test. That's the point.

Notes: Leads with surprising research, connects to relatable experience, offers simple filter
```

### Example 3: The Personal Case Study
```
Subject: The week I stopped checking email before noon
Section: Experiment report
Content:
I ran an experiment last month that felt genuinely uncomfortable.

No email before noon. For one week.

Here's what I expected: missed emergencies, angry colleagues, chaos.

Here's what actually happened:

Day 1: Constant urge to check. Physically uncomfortable.
Day 2: Wrote more in one morning than the previous full week.
Day 3: Realized most "urgent" emails from the morning were already resolved by afternoon.
Day 4: A colleague mentioned I seemed "more present" in our meeting.
Day 5: Extended the experiment indefinitely.

The surprising part wasn't that I got more done. It's that nothing broke. The emergencies I imagined? They didn't exist.

I'm not saying this works for everyone. But I am saying: the assumptions about what we "have to" respond to immediately are worth questioning.

Notes: Vulnerable about discomfort, specific day-by-day details, doesn't overgeneralize
```

## Headline & Title Formulas

### Veritasium-Style (Counterintuitive Hooks)
- "Why [Common Belief] Is Actually Wrong"
- "The [Surprising] Reason [Unexpected Outcome]"
- "What Everyone Gets Wrong About [Topic]"
- "[Unexpected Thing] Is Actually [Counterintuitive Truth]"
- "This [Simple Thing] Changes Everything About [Topic]"

**Examples:**
- "Why Working Harder Won't Make You More Productive"
- "The Surprising Reason Top Performers Take More Breaks"
- "What Everyone Gets Wrong About Morning Routines"
- "Your Calendar Is Actually Making You Less Organized"

### Diary of a CEO-Style (Bold + Specific)
- "[Expert] Reveals: The [Counterintuitive Truth] About [Topic]"
- "The [Number] Things [Successful People] Never Do"
- "[Common Practice]: Why It's Sabotaging Your [Goal]"
- "The Hidden [Cost/Benefit] of [Common Advice]"

**Examples:**
- "Psychologist Reveals: Why Discipline Is Overrated"
- "The 3 Things High Performers Never Put on Their Calendar"
- "Multitasking: Why It's Sabotaging Your Best Work"
- "The Hidden Cost of Always Being Available"

## Voice Calibration Checklist

Before publishing, ask:

### Counterintuitive Test
- [ ] Does this challenge something people assume is true?
- [ ] Is the challenge backed by evidence, experience, or logic?
- [ ] Will readers feel intrigued rather than attacked?

### Vulnerability Test
- [ ] Have I shown my own uncertainty or past mistakes?
- [ ] Am I speaking *with* readers, not *at* them?
- [ ] Would I be comfortable if my team read this?

### Value Test
- [ ] Does this give readers something useful?
- [ ] Is this specific enough to be actionable?
- [ ] Would I share this if someone else wrote it?

### Tone Test
- [ ] Professional but not stiff?
- [ ] Confident but not arrogant?
- [ ] Challenging but not polarizing?
