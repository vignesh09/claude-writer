## LINKEDIN POST:

I spent 3 years building the perfect habit system.

Color-coded spreadsheets. Tracking apps with streak counters. Weekly reviews. Monthly audits.

The result? I got really good at managing my failure.

Here's what surprised me when I looked at the research:

92% of people fail at behavior change within three months. The 8% who succeed aren't using sophisticated systems.

They're obsessively executing three boring tactics everyone else dismisses as "too basic":

- Put running shoes by the bed (environment design)
- Delete social media apps instead of using willpower (friction removal)
- "If I finish my coffee, then I write for 20 minutes" (implementation intentions)

That last one? A meta-analysis of 94 studies found it makes people 3x more likely to complete difficult goals.

Three times more effective. Almost nobody uses it.

Why? Because it feels insufficient. Our brains resist solutions that seem too simple.

The search for the perfect system is itself procrastination wearing a productivity costume.

I know because I lived it. Every hour I spent comparing habit tracking apps was an hour not spent actually doing the thing.

The uncomfortable truth: For most people, the basics aren't a starting point before you "graduate" to complex systems.

They're the actual solution.

What's one "too simple" tactic you've been dismissing?
