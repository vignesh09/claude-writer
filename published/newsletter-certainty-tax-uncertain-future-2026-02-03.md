## EMAIL BODY

I spent six months researching whether uncertainty will increase in the future.

Job churn projections. AI displacement forecasts. Wealth inequality trends. Geopolitical risk indices.

The data was overwhelming. And also, I realized, completely useless.

Here's what finally clicked: trying to predict whether the future will be uncertain is like forecasting the weather for next year's vacation. By the time you know if you're right, it doesn't matter anymore.

The real question isn't "What's going to happen?"

It's "How do I build a life that works regardless of what happens?"

That shift changed everything.

---

### The forecasting problem nobody mentions

When COVID hit, epidemiologists built thousands of prediction models. Research later showed these models had a "dubious track record" at best.

Economists? They're decent at predicting the next quarter. Beyond three months, their accuracy falls apart. They consistently miss major inflection points.

In 2010, experts confidently predicted truck driving would be automated by 2020. As of 2026, the trucking industry still has a driver shortage.

MIT Sloan researchers put it bluntly: "In most areas of business, accurate forecasting is not possible, and future uncertainty is much greater than most managers acknowledge."

So here we are - anxious about predictions made by people whose predictions are provably unreliable.

The irony is brutal.

---

### What I call the certainty tax

Here's the framework I keep coming back to.

The "certainty tax" is the premium people pay to avoid uncertainty.

Consider:
- "Stable" corporate jobs often pay less than portfolio careers with equivalent total compensation. Why? Because employees accept lower wages for perceived security.
- IT workers fantasize about buying farmland and quitting tech - essentially trying to *buy permission to rest* rather than setting boundaries now.
- The specialized degree that leads to one career path might cost you 50-70% in optionality compared to broader skills.

Nassim Taleb captured this: "As an author, nothing I can do that generates attention will reduce the sale of my books. But if you're a midlevel executive at some bank, you're extremely fragile."

The "normal" career path is becoming the *most* automation-vulnerable. Entry-level jobs are disappearing. Middle management is being compressed.

Meanwhile, the "weird" careers - hard to categorize, illegible to HR systems, difficult to automate - are becoming safer.

We're paying premiums for stability that doesn't actually exist anymore.

---

### What this looks like practically

Instead of trying to predict, I've been building options.

Optimization assumes stable conditions. Optionality means having choices *after* events occur.

What shifted for me:
- Don't pick the job with the highest salary. Pick the one that builds the most transferable skills.
- Don't buy the biggest house you can afford. Keep costs low so you can pivot careers if needed.
- Don't specialize so narrowly you're trapped. Build skills that combine in unusual ways.

40% of professionals globally now have multiple income streams. That's not fringe anymore - it's adaptation in real time.

The half-life of professional skills has dropped from 10 years to 5 years. For technical skills, it's below 2.5 years.

But here's the thing: you don't need to learn everything. Focus on skills that *compound* - writing, sales, systems thinking, rapid learning ability. Meta-skills that help you acquire domain skills faster.

---

### The question that actually matters

Will uncertainty increase? Probably. The data points that direction.

But honestly? I don't know. No one does.

What I do know: Predictions fail. Robustness works.

You can spend the next decade anxious about uncertainty, trying to predict and prepare for specific scenarios.

Or you can build a life with enough optionality that you adapt to whatever actually happens.

One approach is exhausting and has a terrible track record.

The other is liberating and has evidence behind it.

The certainty tax is what you pay for the illusion of control.

You can stop paying it anytime you want.
