## EMAIL BODY:

I've been browsing farmland listings for six months.

Every time I hit three back-to-back meetings, or find myself answering Slack at 11 PM, the same fantasy plays: morning coffee watching the sunrise. Organic vegetables. Clean air. No sprint planning.

I'm guessing some version of this lives in your head too.

Here's what stopped me from actually buying: I ran the numbers. And the numbers told a story I wasn't expecting.

Notes: Opens with personal admission of the same fantasy readers have. Establishes credibility through vulnerability before challenging the idea.

### The math nobody does

The average software engineer in India earns 7-8 lakh annually.

The average farmer? 1.2 lakh. When you isolate just farm income, it drops to 27 rupees per day.

That's a 6-7x income reduction.

"But I'd do organic farming," you might think. "Agritourism. Value-added products."

I thought that too. So I looked at the success stories. Every celebrated IT-to-farm transition I found had the same pattern: existing family land, significant capital reserves, or professional agricultural training.

The person who quit their 12-lakh job, bought a weekend plot, and now makes comparable income through farming?

I couldn't find them.

Notes: Specific numbers create credibility. Anticipates reader objections and addresses them. The "I couldn't find them" line is more powerful than saying "they don't exist."

---

### The psychology that made me uncomfortable

Here's where the research got personal.

Psychologists have a term for what we're experiencing: "Grass is Greener Syndrome" (GIGS). It's the pattern of using geographic solutions to solve internal psychological problems.

The clinical literature describes people with GIGS as having difficulty trusting their own decisions because they've repeatedly tried to "escape" situations only to find the same problems in new locations.

This line hit me:

*The inability to set work boundaries in Bangalore doesn't magically disappear when you move to Thally.*

If I can't say no to 70-hour weeks now, what makes me think I'll maintain those boundaries while also managing farm operations, dealing with agricultural labor, and learning cultivation practices?

The farm fantasy offers permission to rest.

But I could grant myself that permission today. In my current job. In my current city.

Notes: GIGS concept is the pivot point. The quoted line is formatted for emphasis. Transitions from external data to internal truth.

---

### What actually works (but isn't Instagram-worthy)

While researching, I found data that barely appears in the "escape to farm" narrative.

Tier-2 cities saw 50%+ year-over-year growth in IT hiring in 2025. Fifteen percent of India's tech talent has already moved to places like Indore, Jaipur, and Coimbatore.

The math: rent at 5,000-10,000 monthly. Established internet. Healthcare access. Real schools.

Cities like these offer 80% of the lifestyle benefits of rural areas with 20% of the infrastructure risk.

But here's why this doesn't dominate the conversation: a photo of your Indore apartment gets "oh, that's nice." A sunrise over your managed farmland gets "goals!"

The farm fantasy offers a narrative of radical life transformation. Tier-2 cities offer practical problem-solving. Our brains prefer the dramatic story, even when the practical solution actually works.

Notes: Evidence-based alternative positioned as "unsexy but effective." The Instagram comparison makes the point memorable.

### What changed for me

I don't have clean answers. I still feel the pull of the farm fantasy when meetings stack up.

But last month, I said no to a Sunday meeting.

It felt terrible. My manager was disappointed. I worried about career consequences.

The meeting got rescheduled. The work still happened. I had Sunday with my family.

That "no" cost me nothing except temporary discomfort.

The farmland I was browsing? 65 lakh, 2-hour weekend commutes, and based on every pattern I found, it would likely remain a weekend retreat while I maintain my Bangalore base anyway.

One of those solves my problem. The other lets me avoid it while feeling productive about "planning my future."

Notes: Returns to personal narrative. The contrast between saying "no" (free) and farmland (65 lakh) is the core insight.

---

### Questions I'm sitting with

Before you spend lakhs on land, have you spent the discomfort of setting actual boundaries?

Is this about farming, or about giving yourself permission to rest - permission you could grant today?

If you can't say no to Sunday emails in Bangalore, why would you suddenly have healthy boundaries while managing farmland?

Are you solving a problem or just changing its location?

The farm will still be there if boundary-setting doesn't work.

But you might be surprised by what changes when you finally give yourself permission to rest without requiring a 60-kilometer commute first.

Notes: Ends with reflection questions, not prescriptive advice. Matches the "curious, not preachy" voice guideline. Final line offers hope without false promises.
